Nurses’ experiences of working under time pressure in care for older persons

Bernadette Dierckx de Casterl´e , Evelyne Mertens , Jessica Steenacker and Yvonne Denier KU Leuven, Belgium

Abstract Background: The international health workforce crisis had led to an increasing shortage of nurses, which has substantial implications for the quality of patient care. This shortage potentially results in nurseperceived time pressure, which can be particularly challenging for nurses who provide care for older persons. Objective: This study aimed to show how geriatric nurses experience working under time pressure, perceive its impact on care and deal with time pressure in daily care. Research design: A qualitative descriptive interview design was used. Participants and research context: Purposive sampling led to the inclusion of 11 nurses from three geriatric nursing wards in two general hospitals in Flanders (Belgium). Data were collected using semistructured in-depth interviews and analysed using the QUAGOL (Qualitative Analysis Guide of Leuven). Ethical considerations: The study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of the University Hospitals Leuven (Ethics committee of the University Hospitals Leuven). Findings: In all interviews, time pressure was described as ubiquitous in the daily care of older persons. A sense of failure in providing care was the common thread in many interviews. Nurses felt compelled to ‘reduce’ good-quality care to basic care by focusing on the physical and visible aspects of care. Nevertheless, personal experiences with time pressure and strategies to cope with it differed among the interviewees. These variations were related to the working environment and the nurses themselves. They underscored the importance of nurses’ perspectives for a good understanding of the phenomenon of time pressure. Discussion and conclusion: Working under time pressure in the care of older persons leads to various important challenges for nursing ethics. The findings show that providing care that promotes the human dignity of older persons in busy working environments in which care is rationed is an important ethical challenge. As such, our study offers a baseline for further research and discussion on how to support nurses working under time pressure.

Keywords Empirical approaches, care of older people, grounded theory, missed care, qualitative research, time pressure, ethics

Introduction

Timepressureisawidespreadphenomenoninnursingpractice.Worldwide,therehasbeenaseriousshortageof nurses, while the need for qualified nursing staff constantly increases.1 This has led to an emerging body of literature on rationing of care in nursing, referring to nurses’ inability to provide all care activities for patients due to lack of time and resources.2 Evidence shows that in situations of severe time constraints, nurses prioritize, which may result in certain aspects of care being delayed or omitted. This has resulted in a growing number of studies on ‘missed nursing care’ or ‘care left undone’ and the impact on patient care.2 A recent systematic review revealed adverse consequences for patients, in particular dissatisfaction with the given care and an increased risk of adverse clinical outcomes such as urinary tract infections, falls and pressure ulcers.3

Time pressure in nursing has been explored mainly from an objective perspective, reducing it to a problem of time allocation, that is, a lack of time to deliver the expected care activities. From this perspective, time pressure is considered an objective, measurable and (perhaps) controllable event.4

However, time pressure is also a subjective phenomenon referring to persons’ awareness of not being able to complete their duties and responsibilities in the time available, which results in a feeling of being rushed.4,5 From this perspective, time pressure involves both experiencing a lack of time (tempo) and limits and choices within one’s responsibilities. This underlines the importance of understanding time pressure from the perspective of nurses and in relation to their specific professional and ethical challenges in care.5,6

However, research on nurses’ experiences of time pressure is relatively scarce. Two studies on nurses’ perceived time pressure in daily work described how nurses felt limited in their ability to provide comprehensive nursing care.7,8 A few studies illustrated how constant time pressure resulted in nurses using various strategies to compensate for a lack of time.8-11 Minimizing time spent on tasks (e.g. by establishing a routine or by prioritizing); creating new time (e.g. by working faster or combining tasks); or, when new time could not be created, redefining working responsibilities (e.g. by assigning work to another shift or by delegating) were strategies that were used.9 The strategies differed from how nurses perceived time pressure, that is, as a trigger to organize nursing care in the best possible way despite a lack of time or as an emergency situation requiring ad hoc solutions to survive.8 Time pressure may force nurses to give up ‘should do’ in favour of ‘must do’ work9 and thus compromise their clinical and ethical values.12

More insight into nurses’ experiences can help us understand how to support nurses in addressing their ethical challenges under time pressure, particularly in providing care for older patient populations. Older patients are described as physically, psychologically and socially frail, and they can also easily feel harmed, hurt and depersonalized. These feelings refer to older persons’ ethical vulnerability.13 Being sensitive to this vulnerability requires an understanding of what they go through and how illness and ageing affect their lives and well-being. Understanding and encountering the deep vulnerability of older persons may be an ethical challenge for nurses working under time pressure.12

The general aim of the study was to contribute to the understanding of time pressure in care for older persons and, more specifically, how nurses caring for older persons (1) experience working under time pressure, (2) perceive its impact on care and (3) deal with time pressure in daily practice.

Method

Design

A qualitative descriptive interview design was chosen, inspired by the grounded theory approach, to explore nurse-perceived time pressure and its underlying dynamics in a geriatric nursing care context.14

Setting and sampling

The study was conducted in 2016 in two general hospitals in Flanders, Belgium. In consultation with nursing managers, we selected geriatric nursing wards free of organizational reforms during the time of the study. This resulted in the selection of 3 units. Participants were selected purposively and had to meet the following criteria: (1) being a bedside nurse; (2) having an associate or bachelor’s degree in nursing; (3) being employed at one of the selected geriatric nursing units; (4) having at least 2 years of work experience in nursing, of which at least 6 months were in the present geriatric unit; and (5) being at least 70% contractually employed. The researcher, a Master of Nursing Science student, visited each unit and provided information about the purpose of the study and its expectations. Nurses who were interested in participating completed a standardized questionnaire inquiring about socio-demographic characteristics, workload and time pressure on the unit. These data were used to facilitate the interview and describe the final sample. All nurses who completed the questionnaire were included in the study. The final sample consisted of 11 women aged 22 to 57 with an average of 11 years of professional experience, of which approximately 7 years were in the geriatric units. The majority had a full-time position. Six nurses had an associate degree, five had a bachelor’s degree, three had a postgraduate degree and one had a master’s degree in nursing. Nurses’ responses to the questionnaire indicated that they often or always experienced time pressure in their daily care practices. They all worried about time pressure and experienced its impact on their care practice. All but one participant indicated that they sometimes or often felt that they could do more for the patient if they had more time (Table 1).

Data collection

Data were collected by means of semi-structured in-depth interviews using an interview guide. The guide was developed based on a literature study and further refined during the research process. The interviewer (a Master of Nursing Science student) opened each interview by asking for more detail about the participants’ answers given on the questionnaire. Next, the participants were asked to describe their most recent workday and, more specifically, the time issues in their care activities. Using open and probing questions, the interviewer invited the nurses to expand on specific cases. According to the participant’s preference, the interview took place in a room at the hospital or at the participant’s home. The interviews lasted approximately 1 h and were audio-recorded - with the participant’s consent - and transcribed verbatim. After transcription, a descriptive and methodological report was prepared for each interview.

Data analysis

Data collection and analysis occurred simultaneously and interactively based on the Qualitative Analysis Guide of Leuven (QUAGOL).15 The guide is inspired by the constant comparative method of the grounded theory approach13 and consists of two parts, each with five stages, to guide the researcher through a cyclic and simultaneous process of data collection and analysis. The interview data were coded and analysed by the interviewer and a second researcher (an experienced qualitative researcher in nursing ethics). The process started with a case-oriented approach that stimulated the researchers to first analyse and understand each case as a whole and develop conceptual schemes of the individual interviews. Using the method of constant comparison, the researchers gradually identified common themes within and across the interviews. A resulting list of themes provided the researchers with a framework for the actual coding using qualitative software (QSR NVivo 11). Finally, a cross-case thematic analysis consistently applied to all data resulted in a meaningful conceptualization of the experience of time pressure in the care of older persons.

Ethical considerations

Results

The nurses reported that they experienced time pressure as an inherent aspect of the nursing profession, especially in a geriatric context. In all interviews, time pressure was described as ubiquitous in the daily care of older persons. Nevertheless, personal experiences with time pressure differed among the interviewees. Some regarded stress and agitation associated with working under time pressure as a normal phenomenon. Others expressed their concerns and frustrations and described working under time pressure as problematic in geriatric care. Differences in experiences were also reflected in how the nurses described their coping strategies. Some strategies were focused on the ‘survival’ of busy periods through the use of available resources and habits. Other strategies involved more proactive ways of dealing with time pressure by looking for solutions to provide good care, notwithstanding time shortage. Variation in nurses’ experiences regarding time pressure was related to the working environment (e.g. culture and work organization) and to the nurses themselves (e.g. their personal values and priorities). The consequences of working under time pressure were illustrated in detail by most of the nurses. A sense of failure in providing care was the common thread in many interviews. The interviewees illustrated how they felt compelled during (acute) time pressure to ‘reduce’ good-quality care to basic care by focusing on the physical and visible aspects of care.

Ubiquity of time pressure in care for older persons

Time pressure was explicitly recognized and extensively illustrated by every participant. Many participants described time pressure as a normal phenomenon in the care of older persons. Working under time pressure was seen as the rule rather than the exception. It sometimes was no longer even questioned.

Oh, I actually think it has become a habit, continuously running from pillar to post. I also think that it is everyone’s personal responsibility. [ . . . ] In all this, I try to search for a balance on my own . . . (3)

Some nurses seemed to minimize the impact of time pressure, particularly at the beginning of the interview. According to them, working under time pressure was mainly seen as an inevitable part of the reality of geriatric care that should not prevent them from providing the necessary care. However, as the interviews progressed, it gradually became clear that the nurses did experience negative impacts of time pressure on care activities.

They implicitly referred to the particular vulnerability of older persons that made it even more harsh to postpone or not provide the necessary care. Although not elaborated in depth during the interviews, the nurses often linked time pressure to changes in modern society. Many of them also experienced time pressure in their private lives, which resulted in a persistent feeling of being rushed. Time pressure experienced during work almost seamlessly blended with that in their private lives.

I often come home very tired. Like now, I am tired. And when I’ll arrive at home later on, I’ll immediately have to start cooking. On Tuesday evening, I have sewing class, so I need to rush, rush, rush to be there on time. Yes . . . I often feel hunted. (5)

Coping strategies to deal with time pressure

The interviewees described several coping strategies for dealing with time pressure in daily care. Further analysis allowed us to distinguish two categories: (1) strategies that enabled nurses to survive moments of high pressure and (2) strategies that mainly focused on searching actively and proactively for a way to provide proper care in the given situation. These categories were not mutually exclusive but rather complementary. Although both strategies were present in the nurses’ accounts, we found that one or the other was predominant in their narratives.

Survival strategies. Nurses who mainly focused on survival strategies tried to keep their heads above water with the resources available. They passively submitted to the situation rather than trying to find a way to change the situation. These strategies focused on ‘survival’ of busy periods by ‘doing what needs to be done’. An effort to ‘increase the pace of work’ was a frequently cited strategy in this context. To complete all their activities within a fixed time, the nurses started to work faster. If necessary, they simply worked longer.

Then you’re looking at the clock like, ‘I have to do this and that and also that and that . . . ’ and then you start to speed up physically and mentally to get it all done, because you . . . well, I’m 48 years old now, I am drilled in the idea that ‘it must be done and it will be done!’ And yes, these people must be washed, that wound care must be done, those pills must be given, they must receive their food, and so on . . . (silence). So little by little, you go into overdrive to get it all done. (5)

Delegating in cases of extreme need or sticking to the usual routine were other examples of survival strategies. To some nurses, doing the work in a routine manner offered structure and support when working under time pressure. Routine disruption, on the contrary, acted as a source of extra stress and time pressure.

Time pressure and routines . . . if you have routines, you don’t have much . . . I mean . . . You know what you have to do, so . . . Normally, you know that you can do it within that time frame because it’s nothing new [It’s routine]. And then you think, ‘Yes, I can do it’. Unless, of course, something comes up, which interferes with that routine, and then the workload increases. (1)

(Pro)active strategies. Other nurses tried to deal with time pressure in a more active way by looking for proactive or creative ways to fulfil their care missions regardless of time pressure. Belief in their own ability to influence the workload and its impact on care was at the heart of these nurses’ strategies. According to them, the conditio sine qua non for working under time pressure was good organization of the caring tasks based on optimal planning of their activities and maintenance of control as much as possible. Careful planning and rescheduling of care activities, co-operation with colleagues, agreement and delegation, setting of priorities, and identification of ways to increase their own efficiency were examples of such strategies.

I like to do a lot myself because then, I am in control over what happens . . . but you also have to - if you don’t have time - learn to distribute the work and reduce the workload for yourself . . . handing tasks over to others so that you can do something else without rushing, while they can continue . . . (7)

‘Prioritizing’ was a frequently mentioned strategy to deal with time pressure. The interviewees explained how they distinguished between urgent and non-urgent care during peak times. Priority was given to the most essential care, most often the prescribed medical-technical care, such as the administration of medication, wound care and infusion therapy. Non-urgent or less urgent activities (e.g. hair washing, nail care, serving coffee) were postponed and sometimes even removed from the schedule. Removing care activities, even hygienic basic care, was very difficult for some nurses.

Perceived impact of time pressure

According to the participants, working under time pressure was not without consequences for the quality of provided care. In most interviews, the impact on care was described explicitly and in detail. Time pressure often resulted in feelings of failure in fully caring for patients. The many examples given by the nurses primarily related to the interactional aspects of care and nurses’ inability to integrate medical-technical aspects into care relationships. Most efforts were targeted at providing care focused on physical aspects (e.g. wound care, administration of medication, hygienic care). Working under time pressure often led to ‘basic care’, in which little attention was given to the intangible and less visible aspects of care; however, the interviewees considered these aspects just as crucial to effectively responding to older persons’ needs. Time pressure made the nurses feel that they were obliged to limit their care to a ‘lower level’, which made them feel they failed to offer good care.

Sometimes, it’s all in the little things. Like giving someone a bath or a full massage with body lotion. Those things are small and seemingly insignificant, but they do make people feel more appreciated and truly cared for. You can do more if you have more time. [ . . . ] You can’t provide care as extensively as when you actually would have time for it. I really regret that . . . (Short silence) I could be a better caregiver if I had more time. (7)

More specifically, the nurses’ stories clearly illustrated how time pressure could negatively affect their interactions with patients and their families. For example, they experienced a decreasing number of opportunities or decreasing lengths of time for conversation with patients, even though they felt that their patients or their families truly needed good conversation. This feeling of failure was reflected most notably in situations where nurses were faced with intense or acute suffering of the patient and/or family, such as, for example, in the case of palliative sedation.

So yes, I gave the injection, and then I had to leave the family on their own . . . let them be there . . . prostrate and perplexed. So sometimes, I find it very sad that we do palliative sedation. The feeling of failure was due not only to the lack of time to respond to the needs of each patient but also to the way that care was offered in situations of time pressure. According to the nurses, patients saw and felt that nurses had no or insufficient time to truly listen or give them undivided and necessary attention. In their opinions, the ‘hurried impression’ that they often gave had negative impacts on patients. Some nurses explicitly mentioned that they perceived that some patients became lonely because nurses had so little time for them.

They say it, or . . . you get the feeling that they want to tell you something, but instead are restraining themselves because they think, ‘They are busy’ . . . Or sometimes they want to ask you something, but instead they say, ‘No, never mind. You already have enough work on your plate’. (10)

Factors affecting time pressure experiences

On a more fundamental level, we identified two types of factors that could explain the variations in the way nurses experienced and coped with time pressure in the care of older persons, namely, factors related to the working environment and personal factors.

Working environment. The experiences of the interviewees seemed to be affected by their working environments, more specifically, by the working culture and organization of the nursing unit. Current visions, rules and expectations helped determine how nurses provided care and made choices in situations of time pressure. The tendency of many nurses to comply consciously or unconsciously with group standards or group dynamics was striking. Pronounced or unspoken expectations of colleagues, implicit rules about what was and was not (or was less) important proved to be a guide for many nurses in setting their priorities in providing care. Going against these standards was usually not discussed or was experienced as very difficult, even though the nurses expressed serious doubts about certain group standards.

Sometimes, I’m really sorry for thinking, ‘Yes, I want to listen, but I actually don’t have the time’. Then I think, ‘Come on, why didn’t I?’ You shouldn’t let the time pressure make you feel rushed, because you actually have a whole day. But yes, I still catch myself being in that drive. Certainly, when some people say, ‘Come on! We must continue!’ you often get carried away by the people around you, while you should actually say, ‘It’s not important; they can also be washed in the afternoon’. (1)

Many interviewees explicitly referred to the prevailing standard that the work must be done at a good, productive pace so that everything that needs to be done is done in due time. The nurses made clear that this was not only expected by colleagues and management but also by themselves. Not passing on unfinished

Personal factors. Differences in experienced time pressure were also related to the nurses themselves: who they are as individuals and professionals and what their values or priorities are at work. Nurses sometimes imposed norms or minimum standards on themselves, which also affected their experiences of time pressure. They described their belief that it was essential for them to complete certain tasks in due time or to meet certain deadlines.

I just do it. I mean, I make sure my job is done! Maybe a little faster than usual . . . and with less communication to the patient . . . But I can’t function in another way, I simply can’t. (9)

Some interviewees clearly illustrated how their personal values concerning good-quality care and the choices they made accordingly played an important role in how they dealt with time pressure. The following quote illustrates how, despite the high opportunity cost of ‘losing time’, they still tried to devote time and attention to things that, in their opinions, were essential for providing good-quality care.

In the end . . . You are talking with the patient for half an hour, and then with the daughter for yet another half an hour. When it’s also very busy at the same time . . . Nevertheless, I think it’s important! Because in the end . . . she’s 90 years old . . . it must be quite an experience when you suddenly get the news, ‘Madam, it’s no longer possible to continue living in your own home. When you are discharged, you will have to be placed in a nursing home’. Even though you might say that one loses a lot of time in such situations [talking to patients and family], I do believe such conversations are important. (2)

Discussion

Time pressure is ubiquitous in daily care for older persons, but personal experiences with time pressure and strategies used to cope with it differed among nurses, underscoring the importance of the subjective dimension of this phenomenon. Working under time pressure involves more than just a lack of time to deliver care. It challenges nurses to find ways to fulfil their care responsibilities. A common thread throughout the interviews was that the nurses experienced time pressure as an essential hindrance to providing good care for older persons. The interviewees described how working under time pressure led to feelings of failure by being compelled to reduce good-quality care to basic

care and not being able to do something about it. As evidenced in previous research on moral distress, when nurses fail to provide the expected ‘good care’ in a broad sense, it threatens their own identity as ‘a good nurse’.16,17

Most participants used strategies explicitly focused on the ‘survival’ of busy periods by ‘doing what needs to be done’ or tried to maintain control by setting priorities in care. This need for ‘survival’ and prioritization often resulted in patients’ emotional, psychological or existential needs being unmet, according to the interviewees. These findings refer to the problem of ‘missed care’ or ‘care left undone’, which has been widely documented in the international literature.2,3,18 Activities that were least rationed were those that were prescribed and related to physical, physiological and treatment-oriented tasks. This finding has also been supported by other studies.2 Time pressure mainly increases the difficulty of providing openended activities in care, referring to the emerging character of the care relationship and the intangible aspects of care in practice.19 It is precisely in this relational context that difficult choices are made and priorities are set in care.17

Contrary to international studies on ‘missed care’,20 our study did not reveal that nurses actively engaged in rationing care. Rationing of care seemed to be based on implicit practices of individual nurses that were partly influenced by the unit’s team culture and the nurse’s personality or values. This finding is in line with a recent discussion paper noting that rationing decisions are often left to nurses without the provision of any explicit frameworks to guide them in this process.20 This not only makes the process ethically burdensome for nurses but also may result in unarticulated forms of rationing that may be difficult to justify.20

Moreover, in addition to having serious implications for patients, rationing of care also poses a threat to the core of the nursing profession.2 The incompatibility between economic rationality in healthcare and nurses’ professional commitment to holistically deliver care poses a challenge to the nursing profession.2

This may result in nurses’ experience of ‘living on a knife’s edge’, which makes acting according to their own ethical values an extra challenge for them.21 A similar challenge was described by Dierckx de Casterl´e in her exploration of nurses’ daily practice from an ethical perspective.17

In the interviews, the nurses implicitly referred to the particular vulnerability of older patients. Life in old age involves losses, limitations and uncertainty that require special sensitivity from nurses to avoid affecting the person’s integrity. Old people’s frailty often also makes their human dignity vulnerable; they feel depersonalized and de-valued.13 Time pressure clearly makes the ethical challenges in the care of older persons even more complex. Particularly noteworthy in our study is the role of team culture, expectations and rules in the way nurses dealt with time pressure in care. Setting one’s own values and norms for good-quality care, rather than accepting those imposed by the group, appeared to be a valuable but challenging strategy to provide good care under time pressure. The finding that nurses tended to comply with group dynamics is in line with research demonstrating nurses’ strong tendencies for conformism, especially in more difficult caring situations.17 It is important to keep in mind that the team culture and organizational climate can facilitate or hinder the achievement of good nursing care under time pressure.10

Strengths and weaknesses

Contrary to most research on time pressure in nursing, this study explored the phenomenon from a subjective perspective. This approach enabled us to explore time pressure in relationship to nurses’ perceived roles and challenges in the daily care of older persons. An important limitation of the study concerns the sampling strategy. Due to difficulties in recruiting nurses who were willing to participate, we could not perform theoretical sampling. This resulted in a relatively small sample of female participants. Possible bias lies in the absence of male participants and

the possibility that some nurses declined to participate because of time issues. The exclusion of nurses with less experience or lower percentage of employment could also have hindered the discovery of important dynamics in nurses’ experiences. However, we did recruit a diverse group of nurses, enabling us to achieve a relevant mix of characteristics and experiences in our sample. The willingness of nurses to share their experiences also had a positive effect on the quality of the interviews, which provided rich and diverse data. The credibility of the results was enhanced by various built-in guarantees, such as peer review and peer debriefing, bracketing, triangulation and an audit trail, which ensured the trustworthiness of the data. The use of the QUAGOL, particularly the method of constant comparison and the continuous stimulation of reflexivity, was of great value in developing theoretical insights that were grounded in the full potential of the interview data.

Conclusion

The study provides a nuanced description of nurses’ perspectives on time pressure, helping us to better understand time pressure in daily care and its relationship with nurses’ professional and ethical challenges in the care of older persons. Providing care that promotes the human dignity of older persons in busy working environments in which care is rationed was identified as an important ethical challenge. This finding underscores the importance of ethical leadership in the care of older persons. Nursing leaders are in a position to advocate for the essential values of nursing and to determine how best to create a culture and context in which nurses can respond to challenges given the inevitable time constraints.
